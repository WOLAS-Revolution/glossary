A

Accompaniment

A food item served with the main component of the meal to provide balance to the dish.

Aeration

Aeration is the process of incorporating air into a food item.

Alcohol

A chemical made by fermenting sugars with specific microorganisms. Alcohol is highly flammable, and will cause intoxication if consumed in sufficient amounts.

See also

  • Microorganism

Allergy

A condition where certain substances, known as allergens, can have an adverse effect on a person's immune system, causing an allergic reaction. Common food allergens include nuts, tree nuts, eggs, dairy products, fish, shellfish, sesame and soy products.

See also

  • Dietary requirements
  • Egg
  • Dairy
  • Shellfish
  • Finfish

Alternate drop

Two or three different dishes are served alternatively around the table. Guests don't specifically order their dish, but are free to swap with their neighbour if they wish.

See also

  • Order

B

Baking

Baking is the process of heating food with hot air, but without the addition of oils.

See also

  • Oil

Ballotine

Traditionally, a ballotine is a deboned leg of poultry, stuffed, trussed and cooked. A ballotine is usually cooked by braising or poaching.

See also

  • Braising
  • Poaching
  • Poultry

Barding

The process of covering a food item in strips of fat while it cooks. The added fat helps keep the food moist, while imparting flavour.

See also

  • Fat (ingredient)

Basting

A technique where a food item is periodically coated with a sauce, marinade or its own juices during the cooking process. Basting during long cooking processes helps keep the meat moist.

See also

  • Sauce
  • Marinating

Batter

A semi-liquid mixture made by combining dry flours with wet ingredients, such as milk or eggs. The batter is often aerated and flavoured with the addition of other ingredients.

See also

  • Flour
  • Egg
  • Aeration

Bien cuit

Well done; thoroughly cooked

Biological contamination

Biological contamination occurs when food is infested with living microorganisms that are able to cause harm if ingested.

See also

  • Microorganism

Biscuit

A small crisp cake made of sweetened dough, rolled and sliced or dropped from a spoon and baked on a flat sheet.

See also

  • Cake
  • Dough

Blanching

To partially cook food in boiling water. This is often followed by refreshing.

See also

  • Refreshing

Blanquette

A French stew of veal, chicken, or lamb that has been blanched, then cooked in stock, thickened with egg yolks and cream, and garnished with mushrooms and pearl onions. The sauce is always white.

See also

  • Veal
  • Blanching
  • Sauce

Boiling

To cook food in a boiling liquid. The liquid may be water (which boils at 100°C), milk, stock, or any other flavoured liquid. The ratio of liquid to food must be 10:1.

See also

  • Milk
  • Stock

Boning

See "Deboning".

See also

  • Deboning

Braise

A cut of meat that has been cooked by braising.

See also

  • Braising

Braising

In most cases, braising is performed on whole cuts of meat, but may also be used on woody vegetables and root vegetables. The ingredients in the braise are added to the cooking vessel, then half-covered with the cooking liquid and placed in an oven set to a low to moderate temperature.

Bread

Bread is a baked food, usually made from dough of flour and water. Most breads are aerated with yeast to make them soft and light.

See also

  • Dough
  • Flour
  • Aeration
  • Yeast

Brining

To soak protein-based foods in salted water. The food is usually soaked for 6-12 hours.

Brûlée

Refers to foods that are sprinkled with sugar and caramelized with a blowtorch, brûlée iron or salamander, forming a hard brittle top.

  • Sugar

Brunoise

A kind of cut: Fine dice 2×2×2mm.

  • Dice

Buffet

A selection of hot and cold food items from each course are made accessible to customers for a fixed fee.

Butter

A dairy product used as a spread, condiment, or ingredient in cooking. Butter is a water-in-oil emulsion, consisting of milk protein, butterfat, and water.

  • Dairy
  • Emulsion

C

Cake

A mixture usually made of flour, fat, eggs, sugar, liquid, and leavening. Some cakes are fried, but most are baked in an oven.

See also

  • Baking
  • Oven

Canapé

Canapés are a kind of appetiser. Canapés are defined by their hard base, such as a cracker, toasted bread, vol-au-vent or puff pastry. This base is then topped with a flavoursome spread and ingredients.

See also

  • Vol-au-vent
  • Puff pastry

Carbohydrate

The body's primary fuel source, and are available as starch or sugar.

See also

  • Starch

Cartilage

A hard, but flexible connective tissue usually found around the joints and ribs of an animal.

Cartouche (action)

To cook foods en papillote, and to prevent loss of moisture during stewing and shallow poaching.

  • Sugar

Cartouche (item)

A circular piece of greaseproof paper used to cover hot liquids as they cool to prevent a skin from forming.

Carving

Carving is the process of slicing and portioning a large cut of meat.

Caul

The lining around the organs in the internal cavity of most mammals. It can be used to wrap and shape meat-based farces.

  • Farce

Chemical

Any substance that is not used for cooking purposes. Often used to clean and sanitise equipment

  • Chemical

All foods are made up of chemical elements. When different chemical elements come into contact with each other, or are heated or chilled, they can change properties. Common chemical reactions in cooking include the Maillard Reaction and Curdling.

Chocolate

Coeliac

Convection

The indirect transferral of heat into a food through a medium.

Counter service

A service style where the customer must place an order at the bar or counter. The order may then be collected from the bar, or may be brought to the table, depending on the establishment.

Crustacean

Daube

Debearding

Deboning

Any specific needs of a customer in regards to the foods they can or cannot eat. These may be based on their personal beliefs, allergies, or health reasons. Customers may have Coeliac, Lactose Intolerant, Vegetarian, Vegan, Halal, Kosher, food allergy, or other dietary requirements.

Dressed meat

A carcass of an animal is considered to be dressed when the head, feet, tail and internal organs have been removed.

E

Egg

Eggs are laid by birds and reptiles. Eggs are liquid, and very high in protein, giving them a large range of culinary uses.

Emulsion

A mixture of two normally unmixable liquids. Emulsions can be made stable by the use of an emulsifier.

En Papillote

Food is put into a folded cartouche made from greaseproof paper and then baked. Steam from the ingredients is trapped in the pouch, aiding in the cooking process.

See also

  • Cartouche (item)

Equipment

Any utensil or machine used to make a task easier, or to automate it entirely.

F

Farce

A protein-based stuffing that is highly seasoned.

Fat (ingredient)

Fats are a kind of oil that are solid at room temperature. Most solid fats are trans-fats or saturated fats.

See also

  • Trans-fat
  • Saturated fat

Fat (nutrition)

Required by the body to absorb nutrients, and plays a major role in cholesterol levels.

Fibre

The indigestible portion of food that can increase or decrease the time food is digested for.

FIFO

A part of stock rotation. Stands for First In, First Out. The stock put in storage first must be used before the stock put in storage after it.

See also

  • Stock rotation

Fillet

A boneless cut or slice of meat, fish or poultry.

Finfish

Finfish are aquatic creatures that swim freely through the water. All finfish have bones and fins.

First class cuts

A cut of meat that has no cartilage, sinew or connective tissue. Examples include eye fillet, rib fillet, and rump.

Flatfish

  • Finfish

Flour

A powder made by grinding cereal grains. Most commonly refers to Wheat Flour.

Folding

To combine a frothy light substance, such as meringue or cream, with a heavier one by using a gentle circular motion, in order not to lose air and reduce volume and lightness.

Food poisoning

Any illness that is caused by consuming contaminated food or drink.

See also

  • Contaminant

Frenching

Frenching is the process of removing the flesh, sinew, and skin from a bone by scraping the bone with a knife. In many instances, this will require a series of cuts around the bone.

Frogging

A preparation technique where the bird is cut down through its ribcage and flattened out. The end result will somewhat resemble a frog. Frogged poultry will cook quicker, and lose less moisture during the cooking process. It also makes whole birds suitable for grilling.

See also

  • Poultry
  • Grilling

Fruit

The sugary-sweet or acidic, fleshy product of a tree or other plant that contains seeds and can be eaten as food.

G

Garnish

A food item used to provide extra visual appeal to a dish.

Gelatin

Gelatin, or Gelatine, is the most common setting agent, used to make liquids semi-solid. It is available in sheet or powder form, and dissolves in water.

Glazing

To give a shiny appearance to a food item using one of several hot or cold methods.

Gluten

A kind of protein found in wheat, rye and barley products. It is made by kneading the two proteins in wheat, gliadin and glutenin with water.

See also

  • Protein

Gratinating

To cook with a covering of buttered crumbs or cheese until a crust or crisp surface forms, usually under a salamander.

H

Halal

Halal is any action that is permitted by Islamic law. Anything that is forbidden is called “Haram”. A Halal diet has strict rules regarding what can and cannot be eaten.

Herb

Herbs are used in small amounts to impart extra flavour to food and enhance the natural flavours of the ingredients. Herbs, unlike vegetables, are not used to add substance to a dish.

See also

  • Vegetable

Hygiene

Hygiene is a set of practices performed for the preservation of health. Following hygiene practices will reduce the incidence of food contamination.

A chemical reaction during cooking that gives browned foods their desirable flavour.

Marbling

Marbling occurs in meat when fat is distributed through the muscle, creating a marbled pattern. The more marbling the meat has, the more flavoursome, juicy, and tender it is. Marbling can be influenced by both selective breeding and the food given to the animal.

Marinating

Marination is the process of soaking foods in a seasoned, often acidic, liquid before cooking, which will tenderise tough cuts & to add moisture. The liquid is called a marinade, and can be made from oil, wine and sugar syrups.

Masking

Microorganism

Any living thing that is too small to be seen with the naked eye, such as bacteria, spores and mould. Most micro-organisms are harmless, and are used to make foods such as cheese, bread, yoghurt and alcohol. Some micro-organisms are pathogens, meaning they can cause illness or produce harmful toxins.
